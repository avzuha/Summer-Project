Traffic Accident Severity Predictions

This project walks through a full data pipeline built around one question: given the conditions around a crash, such as the weather, the time of day and the road features nearby, can the severity of the accident be predicted ahead of time.

The data comes from the US Accidents dataset on Kaggle, a public countrywide traffic accident dataset that has grown to about 7.7 million records covering 49 states. Working with the full 7.7 million rows was not practical for a project of this size, so the team used a subset of 100,000 records, covering California, West Virginia and Ohio, which kept the analysis and modeling manageable while still leaving enough variety in weather, geography and road conditions to work with.

01

Overview

The project follows a straight line from raw data to a working model: load the data, clean it, explore it, train a classifier on it, then measure how well that classifier actually performs.

The source dataset, maintained by Sobhan Moosavi and hosted on Kaggle, is one of the largest public traffic accident datasets available, with about 7.7 million records collected across the United States since 2016. The team pulled a working subset of 100,000 of those records, limited to California, West Virginia and Ohio, so the pipeline below stays fast to run while still reflecting real accident data.

Each stage below is a real step from the project's own notebooks. The charts and numbers shown throughout this page are pulled directly from those notebooks, not recreated separately.

02

Data loading

Before touching the data, the team first looked at what they were actually working with.

The working file is a 100,000 row slice of the larger 7.7 million row Kaggle dataset, holding 46 columns that cover location, timing, weather and road features for each recorded accident. The team checked the shape of the table, the data type of every column, how many values were missing, and how many rows were exact duplicates. This gave a clear picture of what needed to be fixed before any analysis could begin.

03

Cleaning and preprocessing

Raw data rarely arrives ready to model, and this dataset was no exception.

Columns missing more than half their values were dropped entirely. The remaining gaps were filled in, using the median for numeric columns and the most common value for categorical ones. Duplicate rows were removed, and timestamp columns were converted into proper datetime values, which let the team pull the year, month, day, hour and day of week into their own columns for later use.

A few columns that would not help the analysis, such as the free text description and the unique record ID, were dropped. Extreme outliers in numeric fields like temperature, humidity, pressure and wind speed were capped at the 1st and 99th percentile, so that a handful of unusual sensor readings could not distort the results.

04

Exploratory analysis

With clean data ready, the team looked at how accidents are actually distributed, across severity, weather, time and place, before building any model.

05

Model training

Before training, the four severity levels were collapsed into two, since levels 1 and 4 had too few examples on their own to model reliably.

Severity 1 and 2 became "mild" and severity 3 and 4 became "severe". This turned an unbalanced four-class problem into a workable two-class one, without losing the practical meaning of the prediction. Categorical fields such as state, weather condition and wind direction were label encoded, boolean road-feature flags were converted to plain integers, and the data was split into an 80 percent training set and a 20 percent test set. A Random Forest Classifier made up of 100 trees was then trained on the training set.

07

Conclusion

The Random Forest model reached an accuracy of 84.53 percent on the test set, meaning it correctly classified about 85 out of every 100 accidents it had never seen before.

A precision of 81.35 percent means that when the model flagged an accident as severe, it was right the large majority of the time. A recall of 85.01 percent means it also caught most of the truly severe accidents rather than missing them, and an F1 score of 83.14 percent reflects a solid balance between the two. An AUC score of 0.918 points to a strong overall ability to tell mild and severe accidents apart.

Taken together, these results suggest the model works well as a decision support tool, one that could help flag accidents likely to be severe based on conditions such as location, weather and time of day.
